This report contains updated information on West Nile virus (WNV) detections in humans, corvids, and horses across the province of British Columbia (BC). Additionally included are historic findings from when mosquito trapping was conducted annually as part of routine annual WNV surveillance in BC (2003-2014). Mosquito sampling was briefly resumed in 2024 as part of a pilot program but is not routinely conducted as a part of the WNV surveillance program in BC.

This page is updated as positive cases are reported. Report updated on 2025-04-02.


Key message on WNv



West Nile Virus

West Nile virus (WNv) surveillance in British Columbia has been conducted through testing horses and birds that are sick or dead, and humans who have symptoms compatible with WNV. Blood and organ donations are also screened for WNV.

Mosquito Surveillance

From 2003-2014, the BCCDC conducted active mosquito surveillance focused on WNV testing, identification, and distribution of adult mosquitoes to monitor local mosquito populations for WNV. At the time the program was initiated, WNV present in neighboring jurisdictions but not yet present in BC. Active mosquito surveillance was discontinued as a component of the WNV surveillance program after the 2014. Throughout the course of this program, WNV was detected in 11 mosquito pools of Culex tarsalis in the South Okanagan (10 in 2009, 1 in 2013).

In the summer of 2024, ten years after routine mosquito surveillance was last conducted in the province, an 8-week pilot mosquito surveillance program was conducted in the Nanaimo and Osoyoos regions and captured mosquitoes were pathogen tested for Snowshoe hare and Jamestown Canyon viruses of the California serogroup in addition to WNV.

Aedes spp.

Aedes species are typically the most abundant mosquitoes in Canada and are numerous in BC. Aedes were the most collected mosquitoes in all years of the active mosquito surveillance program that ran from 2003 to 2014.
These mosquitoes have a reputation as nuisance pests, especially when flooding occurs, and the amount collected from year to year varied depending on the amount of moisture accumulation and snowmelt.


Anopheles spp.

Anopheles species are well known globally as the primary vector of malaria, although this disease no longer endemic in Canada. Anopheles earlei is one species that can be found across the province and are vicious biters that are tolerant of cold temperatures (Belton 2007).


Culex spp.

Culex species are more often found infected with WNV than any other group of mosquitoes across the North American continent, and the two primary vector species that drive WNV activity in BC are Culex pipiens and Culex tarsalis.
WNV has been detected in eleven Culex tarsalis mosquito pools in the South Okanagan, with the first detection occurring in 2009.


Culiseta spp.

There are several Culiseta species present in BC, the most common of which being Culiseta incidens. This species is well adapted to urban areas and can be found near artificial containers, such as catch basins, as well as drainage ditches.


Coquillettidia spp.

Coquillettidia perturbans is the most well-known Coquillettidia species and can be found near lakes and ponds where aquatic plants, such as cattails, are present. Their larvae live below the surface of the water and water temperature determines their final stages in spring metamorphosis. In BC there is only one generation of Coquillettidia perturbans per year and the adult population was most abundant around the second to third week of July.


Supplementary information

Data notes

The following notes should be considered when interpreting the data:

  • This dashboard reports on the number of new facility outbreaks reported to the BC Centre for Disease Control (BCCDC) by the regional health authorities.
  • Facility outbreaks start dates are determined by the earliest onset date for cases. If the earliest onset date for cases is unavailable, the facility outbreak declared date is used.
  • Data might change from what was previously reported due to updates or data corrections provided by the regional health authorities.
  • Any facility with a reported multi-respiratory-pathogen outbreak, such as a concurrent outbreak of COVID-19 and influenza, will be counted separately for each pathogen in the respective figures.

COVID-19

  • COVID-19 facility outbreaks may be declared at the discretion of the MHO or their official designate. See below for more information regarding outbreak management.
    • COVID-19 Outbreak Management Protocol for Long-Term Care and Seniors’ Assisted Living
    • COVID-19: Outbreak Management Protocol for Acute Care Settings

Influenza

  • There were zero influenza outbreaks reported during the 2020/2021 season.
  • Data from the 2019/2020 to 2021/2022 were excluded from the historical average calculations due to the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • The BCCDC Public Health Laboratory (PHL) conducts the majority of influenza subtyping across the province, including primary specimens that were submitted directly to the BCCDC PHL for influenza diagnosis, and specimens that were positive for influenza at external sites for which secondary subtyping was requested.
  • Starting in the 2023/2024 respiratory season (August 27, 2023 onwards), influenza A subtyping in long-term care facility outbreaks is reported where data are available. In acute care facility outbreaks, influenza A subtyping is reported as unknown.

Epidemiological week/epi-week: A standardized method of counting weeks to allow for the comparison of data year after year. It is a 7-day period that starts on a Sunday and ends on a Saturday. Epi-week numbers and corresponding dates are available here.

Influenza facility outbreak: Two or more influenza-like illness cases within a 7-day period, with at least one laboratory-confirmed case of influenza.
